Case Study #1

Streamlining Grade and Attendance Tracking for NYCDOE: A UX Research and Product Design Case Study

Executive Summary

The New York City Department of Education (NYCDOE), overseeing over 1 million students, faced a critical challenge when pandemic-related lockdowns forced the shift to at-home learning. Amid this transition, the lack of a unified system for tracking grades led to widespread data losses, causing frustration among students, parents, and staff. This case study documents the end-to-end UX research, design, and development process behind a new, integrated educational suite for grade and attendance management, delivering a streamlined, reliable solution.

Problem Analysis

The NYCDOE’s decentralized approach allowed schools to independently select third-party grading software, resulting in incompatible systems. During the 2020 academic year, this fragmentation led to tens of thousands of grades being misplaced or lost. My goal was to design and deliver a unified, NYCDOE-managed platform to securely manage grades and attendance, reducing reliance on external tools and ensuring data consistency.

At the onset of the COVID-19 pandemic, IO Classroom and Google Classroom were the primary software platforms utilized by teachers and administrators at the NYCDOE. However, the abrupt transition to in-home schooling and lack of a centralized process for record keeping highlighted significant vulnerabilities, resulting in the loss of tens of thousands of student records.

Project Scope and Role

As the Senior UX/Product Designer, I collaborated closely with internal stakeholders, including NYCDOE’s Information Technology, Academic Policy, and Chief Information Officer, to establish a strategic development roadmap. I led a small cross-functional team, providing UX and design expertise, from initial research through to production release.

User-Centered Design Approach

We identified key user groups—teachers and school administrators—as primary end-users. Initial requirements were to develop a grade-entry and attendance-tracking tool with essential functionalities, such as course and student management, assignment tracking, grading customization, and reporting capabilities.

Challenges:

  1. Balancing rapid development demands with the need for a user-centered product.
  2. Creating a scalable solution that would allow for future expansion to attendance and other student data functionalities.

Methodology and Research

Stakeholder and User Interviews: To inform the design, I collaborated with an NYCDOE administrator who helped assemble a group of "super users" for roundtable discussions. These sessions provided direct insights into user pain points, unmet needs in current software, and key motivations for transitioning to a new platform. Additionally, I conducted competitor analysis, evaluating similar tools to identify best practices and areas for improvement.

Key Findings:

  • Competitor products lacked customizability in grading and data import.
  • Teachers valued seamless integration with NYCDOE databases and cross-platform compatibility.
  • A mobile-friendly design was essential for classroom use, especially for attendance tracking.

Design Strategy

Wireframes & User Flow Mapping: After sketching initial concepts, I created low-fidelity wireframes to establish foundational user flows. Rapid stakeholder feedback on these designs allowed us to make swift adjustments before advancing to higher fidelity. Low-fidelity wireframes included interactive elements to represent key workflows, enhancing clarity and alignment with development.

High-Fidelity Mockups and Design System: With stakeholders' approval, I moved to high-fidelity wireframes, defining a consistent design system that would support the gradebook, attendance tracking, and future features. The high-fidelity designs included complete user flows, interactions, and layouts for each application element, ensuring a cohesive experience across both desktop and mobile.

Iterative Development and Adaptation

Shift in Requirements: Mid-development, I received an urgent request to integrate attendance tracking into the initial release, requiring quick turnaround. By leveraging my established design system, I expedited high-fidelity mockups for the attendance module and introduced a mobile-first layout to accommodate in-class use on tablets and mobile phones.

Dark Mode Introduction: Given its practicality for classroom environments, I implemented a dark mode feature, a first for NYCDOE applications, which received enthusiastic feedback.

Launch & Result

The project culminated in the successful launch of a fully integrated application—"Gradebook and Attendance." The feedback from stakeholders and users was overwhelmingly positive, with smooth adoption rates attributed to the unified system’s reliability and ease of use. Teachers and administrators appreciated the consolidated platform, streamlined workflows, and reduced dependency on external software. Most importantly, the NYCDOE had an application that safeguarded student data by implementing one single platform to store and record student records.

Conclusion

This project underscores the value of user-centered design in large-scale educational systems. By maintaining a strong focus on user research and iterative feedback, I delivered a product that not only met but exceeded the expectations of both end-users and stakeholders. The NYCDOE’s flagship addition to its new in-house education suite now serves as a model for scalable, cohesive educational tools.

Case Study #2

UX Case Study: Transforming the NYCDOE Application Hub

Executive Summary

The New York City Department of Education (NYCDOE) sought to transform a small, outdated application into a centralized platform for students, parents, and teachers. The project aimed to integrate essential features like attendance, grades, assignments, student profiles, and messaging, while modernizing the user experience (UX) and interface (UI). As the Senior UX/Product Designer, I led the effort to revamp the platform, creating a seamless, user-friendly experience tailored to a diverse audience ranging from young children to senior educators.

Problem Analysis

The existing application hub, originally designed for limited use, became outdated and clunky, making it inadequate for its new expanded purpose as the central interaction point for all stakeholders. The platform needed a complete overhaul to meet modern usability standards and support a vastly broader audience.

Key Challenges

  1. Outdated Design: The UI and UX were not intuitive or visually appealing.
  2. Expanded User Base: The platform had to cater to a wide demographic, from Pre-K students to educators and administrators in their 70s.
  3. Complex User Flows: Navigation was cumbersome, with unnecessary steps and redundant categories.

Team & Role

As the Senior UX/Product Designer, I collaborated closely with key stakeholders, including the Director of the Division of Instructional and Information Technology and the Program Manager. To ensure design vision aligned with technical implementation, I worked closely with the Lead Software Engineer and Front-End Developer.

Responsibilities:

  • Conducted user flow analysis and identified pain points.
  • Designed and implemented a complete UI/UX overhaul.
  • Advocated for a mobile-first approach to prioritize the needs of the majority user base.

Project Kickoff: Defining Priorities

Users

The end users included:

  • Students: Ages 3–18, with varying levels of digital literacy.
  • Parents: A diverse group with different levels of tech familiarity.
  • Teachers and Administrators: Ranging from tech-savvy professionals to older educators less comfortable with technology.

Core Priorities

  1. Modernize the Interface: Create an appealing, modern, and accessible design.
  2. Streamline User Flows: Simplify navigation for users of all ages and abilities.
  3. Mobile-First Design: Focus on usability for phones and tablets, as these were the primary devices for most users.
  4. Reduce Complexity: Eliminate redundant categories and pages to improve ease of use.

Design Process

User Flow Analysis

I began by mapping out the current user flows, identifying inefficiencies and unnecessary steps. This allowed me to pinpoint areas for improvement, including:

  • Overly complex navigation menus.
  • Redundant categories leading to user confusion.
  • Poor integration of essential features like messaging and assignments.

Key Areas of Improvement

From this analysis, I outlined actionable changes:

  1. Redesign the UI Completely: Introduce a modern, visually cohesive interface.
  2. Streamlined Navigation: Replace the old navigation structure with a clean, intuitive navigation bar.
  3. Flexible Viewing Options: Add a list view and grid view toggle to suit different user preferences.
  4. Simplified User Flows: Reduce the number of pages and streamline categories for quicker access.

High-Fidelity Wireframes

Given the stakeholders’ familiarity with the existing platform, I skipped low-fidelity mockups and moved directly to high-fidelity wireframes. These designs emphasized:

  • Mobile-First Design: Ensuring optimal functionality and usability on smaller screens.
  • Immediate User Testing: Allowing stakeholders and users to visualize and interact with the revamped user flow.

Production & Implementation

Complete Overhaul

The application hub was reimagined from the ground up:

  • User Flow: Simplified and streamlined for intuitive navigation.
  • User Interface: Modernized with a sleek, cohesive design system.
  • Mobile Optimization: Delivered a responsive, mobile-first experience that worked seamlessly across devices.

Stakeholder Feedback

Throughout the process, I maintained an iterative design cycle:

  • Presented high-fidelity wireframes to stakeholders for feedback.
  • Incorporated user input to refine flows and interactions.
  • Collaborated with developers to ensure designs translated effectively to production.

Final Results

The Transformed Application Hub

The final product introduced:

  1. Modern Design: A polished, visually engaging interface.
  2. Enhanced Usability: Simplified navigation and customizable views tailored to user preferences.
  3. Mobile-First Experience: Optimized performance on phones and tablets, meeting the needs of the majority of users.
  4. Unified Functionality: Seamlessly integrated features like grades, attendance, messaging, and assignments into one cohesive platform.

Key Outcomes

  • Improved User Satisfaction: Users across all demographics reported a significantly improved experience.
  • Higher Engagement Rates: Increased use of features like messaging and assignment tracking.
  • Streamlined Workflows: Reduced time spent navigating the application and accessing critical information.

Reflections & Lessons Learned

What Worked Well

  • User-Centered Focus: Prioritizing accessibility and simplicity ensured the platform worked for everyone.
  • Stakeholder Collaboration: Frequent feedback loops led to a product that aligned with both user needs and organizational goals.

Opportunities for Future Enhancements

  • Continuous User Testing: Iterative testing can uncover further areas for improvement.
  • Scalability: Design principles can extend to other NYCDOE applications.

Conclusion

This project demonstrates the impact of empathetic, user-centered design on transforming outdated systems into intuitive, modern platforms. By focusing on accessibility, simplicity, and functionality, I delivered a product that exceeded user expectations and set a new standard for NYCDOE applications.

Case Study #3

UX Case Study: Developing a Confidential Reporting Tool for Executive Stakeholders

Executive Summary

This case study outlines the UX design and development of a groundbreaking reporting tool tailored for senior stakeholders, including directors, VPs, and chairpersons. The application serves as a centralized platform for accessing highly confidential information, catering to both technical and non-technical users. As a greenfield project, it required innovative solutions to address unique challenges in usability, security, and data visualization.

Background

Overview

A federal agency sought to revolutionize how its executive team accessed and interacted with critical data. Existing systems were fragmented, insecure, and lacked user-friendly interfaces, leading to inefficiencies and potential security risks.

Project Goals

  • Unified Platform: Develop a single application consolidating all essential reports and data.
  • User-Friendly Interface: Ensure accessibility for non-technical users without compromising advanced features for technical stakeholders.
  • High Security: Implement robust security measures to protect sensitive information.
  • Innovative Reporting: Introduce advanced data visualization and interactive reporting capabilities.

Challenges

Diverse User Base

  • Technical Variance: Users ranged from tech-savvy analysts to non-technical executives.
  • Different Needs: Varied objectives, from in-depth data analysis to high-level overviews.

Security Requirements

  • Confidential Data: Handling highly sensitive government information dealing with national security.
  • Regulatory Compliance: Adherence to regulations and internal policies.

Usability vs. Complexity

  • Complex Data: Presenting complex datasets in an understandable manner.
  • Ease of Use: Simplifying interactions without oversimplifying the data.

User Research

Stakeholder Interviews

Conducted one-on-one interviews with:

  • Directors and Chief-of-Staff: To understand high-level needs and decision-making processes.
  • Technical Teams: To grasp the technical requirements and data intricacies.

Key Insights:

  • Directors required quick access to key performance indicators (KPIs) without navigating complex menus.
  • Technical users needed advanced features like data drilling and custom report generation.

Surveys and Questionnaires

Distributed surveys to a broader audience to gather quantitative data on user preferences and pain points.

Findings:

  • 70% of non-technical users found existing tools cumbersome.
  • 85% expressed concerns over data security in current systems.

Contextual Inquiry

Observed users interacting with existing systems to identify workflow bottlenecks and usability issues.

Design Process

Defining User Personas

Created detailed personas representing:

  • Non-Technical Executives: Focused on strategic decision-making.
  • Technical Analysts: Required detailed data manipulation capabilities.

Information Architecture

  • Sitemap Development: Structured the application to allow intuitive navigation.
  • Content Hierarchy: Prioritized information based on user needs, ensuring critical data is readily accessible.

Wireframing

  • Low-Fidelity Wireframes: Sketched initial layouts to map out key screens and user flows.
  • Feedback Loop: Iterated designs based on stakeholder input.

Prototyping

  • Interactive Prototypes: Developed high-fidelity prototypes using tools like Figma.
  • Usability Testing: Conducted tests with representatives from each user group.

Usability Test Results:

  • Improved task completion rates by 40%.
  • Reduced time to access key reports by 50%.

Key Design Solutions

Adaptive Interface Design

  • Role-Based Dashboards: Customized dashboards depending on the user's role.
  • Modular Components: Allowed users to personalize their interface with widgets relevant to their needs.

Simplified Navigation

  • Mega Menu: Introduced a consolidated menu with clear categories.
  • Quick Access Toolbar: Enabled users to bookmark frequently accessed reports.

Advanced Data Visualization

  • Interactive Charts: Implemented dynamic charts that users could manipulate for deeper insights.
  • Drill-Down Features: Allowed technical users to delve into underlying data layers.

Security Features

  • Multi-Factor Authentication: Enhanced login security.
  • Encryption: End-to-end encryption of data in transit and at rest.
  • Audit Trails: Monitored user activity for compliance and security purposes.

Development Collaboration

Agile Methodology

  • Sprint Planning: Coordinated with development teams in bi-weekly sprints.
  • Regular Stand-Ups: Maintained open communication to address challenges promptly.

Design Systems

  • Component Library: Created a reusable library to ensure consistency.
  • Style Guides: Documented design principles, color schemes, typography, and interaction patterns.

Testing and Iteration

Accessibility Testing

  • Ensured compliance with WCAG 2.1 standards.
  • Features included keyboard navigation and screen reader compatibility.

Security Testing

  • Penetration Testing: Engaged third-party security experts to identify vulnerabilities.
  • User Permissions Testing: Validated role-based access controls.

User Acceptance Testing (UAT)

  • Gathered feedback from a pilot group of users.
  • Made iterative improvements based on real-world use cases.

Launch and Adoption

Training and Onboarding

  • Developed user guides and tutorial videos.
  • Hosted webinars and Q&A sessions for different user groups.

Feedback Mechanisms

  • Integrated feedback forms within the application.
  • Established a support channel for ongoing assistance.

Adoption Metrics

  • Achieved a 90% adoption rate within the first three months.
  • User satisfaction scores increased by 60% compared to previous tools.

Results and Impact

  • Enhanced Decision-Making: Executives reported faster and more informed decisions due to easy access to critical data.
  • Improved Efficiency: Reduced time spent on report generation and data retrieval.
  • Strengthened Security: No security breaches reported post-implementation, with data access fully compliant with internal policies.

Lessons Learned

Early Stakeholder Engagement

  • Involving stakeholders from the outset ensured alignment with business goals and user needs.

Balancing Complexity and Usability

  • Simplifying the user experience without sacrificing functionality is key, especially for applications serving a diverse user base.

Importance of Security in UX

  • Integrating security features seamlessly into the user experience enhances trust and adoption.

Conclusion

The successful delivery of this confidential reporting tool showcases the power of user-centered design in addressing complex challenges. By focusing on the needs of both technical and non-technical users and prioritizing security without compromising usability, I provided a solution that not only met but exceeded the expectations, setting a new standard for internal reporting tools.
